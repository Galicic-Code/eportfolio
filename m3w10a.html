Hi Antonios,

Thank you for a very interesting post. I would like to highlight two items: bias in scoring, and the depth of the results.

Scoring bias

We can perhaps assume that different vulnerability scores in CVSS are based on a bias, similar to the biased risk analysis Hunt & Naweed (2023) researched in a workshop setting. The first is cognitive bias (as a result of conscious or sub-conscious mental processes) and the second is motivational bias (which impacts risk judgment). This can lead to understated, skewed risk estimates (Hunt & Naweed, 2023). It would be interesting to see if this can be applied to cyber risk management environment, in the same way as in the ‘traditional’ industries, such as chemical industry, oil & gas, mining, etc.

Depth

Instead of a 10-point scale, the CVSS scale perhaps needs a different approach, for example, the principle of pairwise comparison weighted scores, like in NASA-TLX (Hart & Staveland, 1988), used for workload assessment. NASA-TLX has been scientifically proven numerous times and has been applied in multiple safety critical domains since its beginning. The weighted results of the six dimensions can provide more depth and context to each assessed dimension (i.e., scale), and this would perhaps add more depth to the application of CVSS.

Conclusion

I agree with you that CVSS needs further improvement with scientific justifications. Hopefully numerous trials will be able to improve it.

References

Hart, S.G. and Staveland, L.E. (1988) Development of NASA-TLX (Task Load Index): Results of empirical and theoretical research. In Advances in psychology (Vol. 52, pp. 139-183). North-Holland

Hunt, D. and Naweed, A. (2023) The risk of risk assessments: investigating dangerous workshop biases through a socio-technical systems model. Safety science, 157, p.105918.
