Week 1 Artefact: Discussion Forum 1 Initial Post

Posted on 15 March 2023

Industry 4.0 refers to robotisation, digitisation and industry automation (Vrchota, 2019), with multi-device and meta-data interconnectivity through the concepts such as Cloud Computing, Artificial Intelligence, Big Data, etc., without physical limitations (Kovaitė & Stankevičienė, 2019). On the one hand, Small and Medium sized Enterprises (SMEs) benefit from access to global markets, which lead to increased export (Kovaitė & Stankevičienė, 2019) and higher and more precise productivity, due to automated algorithms, improved quality control, advanced sensors, web-enabled devices, etc. (IBM, n.d.). On the other hand, digitalisation challenges present risks to small sized SMEs due to investment limitations into automation, smart factories, and IT/OT compatibility (IBM, n.d.).

Business risks include inability to adapt to fast-changing systems, lack of staff competencies, limited financial capabilities, challenging dynamic global markets as well as cyber security issues, such as data and network security, and exposure to cyber-attacks (Kovaitė & Stankevičienė, 2019; Kovaitė et al., 2019)

Zimmermann (2016) identified similar top three issues among German SMEs: high operating costs and limited investment capabilities, issues with data protection and data security, and shortage of staff competencies. Digitalisation is often funded internally, with small amounts, exactly due to these issues (Zimmermann, 2016). Additional financing and government grants would accelerate digitisation and reduce challenges SMEs are facing.
